

# Coding Against the Odds: My Reflection as an Observer

The common perception of computer science as a male-dominated field is not what the actual history of computer programming demonstrates when you watch this presentation from Group 4. It was wonderful to learn that history has some truly remarkable female pioneers of computing. For example, Ada Lovelace wrote the first computer algorithm, while Grace Hopper produced the earliest compilers for programming languages. Unfortunately, over time the world of computing has changed, and societal views and stereotypes about computers and geek culture have evolved, causing many women to feel like they have been excluded from the field.

As I watched this presentation, I came to understand that the gender gap in technology is the result of more than just a lack of talent and ability. Another significant factor is the lack of equal opportunity and lack of confidence among females who are interested in pursuing careers in technology. Men generally receive encouragement and earlier access to computers and computer-related activities than girls when they are young. This disparity creates a large difference in "self-efficacy," which is how much a person believes they are capable of accomplishing something. Because of the negative stereotypes regarding women in technology and the lack of female role models for them to look up to, many women deal with feelings of imposter syndrome. Many now doubt themselves when applying for jobs in the industry and, based on feeling alone, may leave the tech field completely.

The team did a great job by demonstrating how this subject is important. The design of technology that excludes women limits our creativity and innovative ideas. Because women have the insight into their community needs, they can prevent the creation of systems that do not consider many of the historical issues faced by women.
